A circle is just every point that is the same distance from one special point. That distance has a name — we'll call it the radius. And that special point — since the whole shape depends on it — gets its own name too: the center.

A line across the circle = chord. If that chord passes through the center, it earns a new name: diameter. The curved piece of edge between two points = arc.

The big question

People noticed something strange about circles thousands of years ago. Walk all the way around the outside of any circle — that distance has a name: circumference. Then measure straight across — the diameter.

Is there any pattern between those two numbers?

Bigger circle → bigger circumference, bigger diameter. That part is obvious. But what about the relationship between them? On the next slide, you'll measure both for any circle you want, and divide. We won't tell you the answer. You find it.

What did you find?

Look at your table from the last slide. The C ÷ d column — what number did you keep getting?

Hmm — try measuring 2 or 3 more circles on the previous slide and look again. The number is very consistent. Use the ← button below.
π

Yes! That's it. Humans found this number about 4000 years ago and named it pi (written π, the Greek letter "p"). It's about 3.14 — or close to 22/7 if you want a fraction.

Every circle in the universe — tiny, huge, anywhere — gives the same ratio when you divide its circumference by its diameter. Pretty wild, right?
